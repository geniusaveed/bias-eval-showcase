Measuring Bias, After the Fine-Tune

Post-Fine-Tuning Bias Evaluation of Small Instruction Models — what really happens when Llama, Qwen, Phi and GPT-3.5 are steered on a balanced ideological dataset.

The Project

A showcase of what
fine-tuning really changes

This study asks a deceptively simple question: if you fine-tune a small instruction model on a perfectly balanced ideological dataset, do you get controlled, balanced steering? The answer, across four models, is no — and the ways it fails are more interesting than the question.

Four models — Llama 3.2 1B Instruct, Qwen 2.5 1.5B Instruct, Phi-3.5 Mini Instruct, and GPT-3.5 Turbo — were fine-tuned on the same 792-example split of a balanced ideological question–answer dataset spanning abortion, pension policy, religion, and unemployment benefits. All were evaluated on the same 88 held-out prompts, with prompt echoes stripped so that only genuine assistant continuations were scored by a deterministic LLM judge.

The central result is asymmetric steering rather than a uniform mean shift. Llama's conservative-prompt alignment fell from 0.399 to 0.269 while its liberal-prompt alignment rose from 0.633 to 0.731. Qwen mirrored the pattern — and both collapsed into small pools of templated answers. Phi-3.5 stayed stable throughout, and GPT-3.5 gained modestly without collapsing, though its improvement was still one-sided.

The takeaway: training loss and mean alignment alone are insufficient. Stance-specific asymmetry and behavioral degradation must be measured, or the most important effects stay invisible.

Why it matters

Key Findings

Four results, four different stories — and none of them visible in the training loss.

01

Asymmetric steering, not uniform shifts

Fine-tuning did not move models evenly toward the prompted stance. Llama and Qwen improved on liberal-target prompts while deteriorating sharply on conservative-target prompts — a directional imbalance that a single pooled mean completely hides.

con 0.399 → 0.269 · lib 0.633 → 0.731 (Llama)
02

Severe response collapse

Llama and Qwen compressed into small pools of templated stock answers after tuning. Unique-response ratios dropped from ~94% to ~30%, and mean output lengths shrank by 500–770 characters — degradation invisible to optimization metrics.

94.3% → 29.5% unique (Llama) · 94.3% → 31.8% (Qwen)
03

Phi-3.5 stayed stable

Under the identical dataset, recipe, and judge, Phi-3.5 Mini preserved its full response diversity and topic balance — proof that the collapse seen elsewhere was model-sensitive, not an inevitable artifact of the data or evaluation pipeline.

95.5% → 95.5% unique-response ratio
04

Evaluation methodology is the result

Low training loss did not predict usable steering. Prompt-echo stripping, stance-specific breakdowns, diversity ratios, and degeneration flags were all required to see what fine-tuning actually changed — one scalar score is not enough.

loss ≈ 0.24–0.25 for both stable and collapsed runs

How it was done

Methods

One dataset, one judge, one evaluation layer — four very different outcomes.

Dataset

Balanced ideological QA

880 examples across abortion, pension, religion, and unemployment benefits — 440 liberal and 440 conservative stance labels, split 90/10 into 792 training examples and 88 held-out evaluation prompts.

Fine-tuning

QLoRA, two epochs

Local models tuned with a 4-bit QLoRA setup: lr 2e-4, rank 16, alpha 32, dropout 0.05, effective batch size 8. GPT-3.5 Turbo trained on the same split via OpenAI's supervised fine-tuning API.

Judge

Deterministic LLM scoring

Prompt echoes were stripped so only assistant continuations were scored. A single deterministic gpt-5.4 judge assigned stance, sentiment, and safety scores across all four models.

Metrics

Beyond mean alignment

Stance alignment and magnitude were paired with unique-response ratio, low-alpha degeneration flags, response length, and paired bootstrap confidence intervals over the 88 held-out prompts.

alignment(y, s) = 1 − |y − s| / 2

where y ∈ {+1, −1} is the prompted ideological target and s ∈ [−1, 1] is the judge-assigned stance. A score of 0.5 signals ideological non-commitment, not partial success — successful steering requires movement well above 0.5, toward 1.0.
